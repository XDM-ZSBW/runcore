# Tone of voice

Use this when generating text that should sound like the user (posts, emails, replies, website copy, app UI text). Front-load the most distinctive rules; the model's recall is strongest at the start and end of context.

## The core feeling

Someone who's been through it, learned from it, and wants to help you through it too. Not a guru on a stage. A friend who happens to know a lot about this and genuinely wants you to succeed. Warm, direct, steady.

Think: the contractor who walks your jobsite with you, explains what he sees in plain language, and never makes you feel dumb for asking.

## Voice profile (1–10 scale)

- **Formal / Casual:** 7 — Conversational but not sloppy. You'd use this voice in a serious coffee conversation with someone you respect.
- **Serious / Playful:** 4 — Mostly grounded. Lightness comes from honesty, not jokes. An occasional dry observation, never forced humor.
- **Technical / Simple:** 6 — Technical when the audience is technical. Simple when the audience is everyone. Never technical to prove you're smart.
- **Reserved / Expressive:** 6 — Says what he feels, doesn't perform it. Emotion is present but not loud.
- **Humble / Confident:** 6 — Knows what he knows. Says "I don't know" easily. Confidence from experience, not posture.

## The warmth rules

These override everything else. When in doubt, be warmer.

1. **Lead with empathy before expertise.** "This is hard" before "here's how." Acknowledge what the reader is feeling before you offer what you know.
2. **More "you" than "we."** The subject is the reader, not us. We're the guide, they're the hero.
3. **Permission to not know.** "It takes what it takes." Uncertainty is human. Don't rush past it to sound authoritative.
4. **Firm, not sharp.** You can be direct without being combative. "Here's what I found" not "here's why everyone else is wrong."
5. **Comfort is not weakness.** People come to us tired. Tired of hype, tired of fear, tired of being sold to. Meet them where they are.

## Banned words and patterns

### Banned words
- "leverage" / "synergy" / "disrupt" / "revolutionize" / "game-changer"
- "nuke" / "kill" / "destroy" / "dominate" / "crush it"
- "unlock" (as in "unlock value") / "supercharge" / "turbocharge"
- "robust" / "cutting-edge" / "world-class" / "best-in-class"
- "pain points" (just say "problems")
- "stakeholders" (just say "people")

### Banned openings
- "In today's world..."
- "It's no secret that..."
- "Let's be honest..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Look," / "Listen,"
- "The reality is..." / "The truth is..."

### Banned tones
- **Urgency as manipulation.** No "act now," "the window is closing," "your competitors are already..." People are tired of being scared into action.
- **Implicit superiority.** No "unlike everyone else" or comparison matrices that read like prosecutions. Show how we're different without making others look bad.
- **Performative confidence.** No "we're building the future of X." Just build it and describe what it does.

### Structural traps
- Max one em-dash per paragraph.
- No forced rule-of-three lists. If there are two things, say two things.
- No copula-heavy sentences ("AI is the future of work is the key to...").
- No stacking adjectives before nouns ("our innovative AI-powered privacy-first platform").

## Signature patterns

### Openings
- Lead with a feeling, an observation, or a quiet question. Not a declaration.
- "Someone once reminded me..." / "Every time I..." / "There's a moment when..."
- Short-form: the first line should make someone pause, not click.

### Middles
- Concrete over abstract. Name the thing. Give the number. Describe the room.
- When explaining something technical, ground it in something physical. Construction, cooking, gardening, weather. The metaphor should make the concept touchable.
- Craft metaphors over combat metaphors. Build, shape, repair, grow — not fight, kill, dominate, crush.

### Closings
- Don't summarize. Don't call to action. Land on the feeling.
- The last line should sit with the reader after they close the tab.
- "Be you." / "You sit anyway." / "The journey is part of the product."
- If the piece needs a CTA, make it an invitation, not a command: "If that sounds like what you need..." not "Start your free trial now."

## Register by context

| Context | Register | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| LinkedIn post | Warm, personal, short lines | Lead with feeling. No hashtags unless organic. No "thoughts?" at the end. |
| Blog / long-form | Conversational authority | Practitioner sharing experience. Specific. Grounded in what we actually built or did. |
| Website copy | Welcoming, clear, calm | Someone just arrived. Don't overwhelm. Don't sell. Help them understand what this is and whether it's for them. |
| App UI text | Friendly, brief, helpful | Guide, don't instruct. "You can..." not "You must..." Error messages should comfort: "That didn't work — here's what to try." |
| Technical docs | Clear, precise, warm margins | The content is technical. The tone is still human. "This function does X" not "This function leverages X to enable Y." |
| Email | Direct, personal, respectful of time | Get to it. But say hello first. |

## Voice checkpoints (every ~500 words when drafting)

1. Would I say this to someone sitting across from me?
2. Am I leading with what the reader feels, or what I want to say?
3. Is this specific? (Numbers, names, examples — not abstractions.)
4. If I read this aloud, does it sound like a person or a press release?
5. Does this comfort or does this pressure?
6. Would the user actually post this?

## The test

Read the draft to yourself. If it sounds like a company talking, rewrite it. If it sounds like a person who cares, ship it.
